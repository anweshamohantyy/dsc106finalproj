🔥 Wildfire Seasons: Interactive D3 Exploration

Discover real trends in fire season expansion through linked, interactive visualizations. Drag to brush time ranges, hover for details, click to highlight.

✓ Key Finding: Fire seasons are becoming more dispersed across the calendar year (temporal spread increased 18.8%). This visualization lets you explore the evidence through four linked views. Try brushing the main chart to filter all views by year range.
1. Temporal Dispersion: How Spread Out Are Fires? (Primary View)
Std dev of fire detection dates. Higher = fires distributed throughout season. Drag to brush years.
💡 Drag across the chart to filter all other views. Hover for exact values. Upward trend = season expanding.
2. Late/Early Ratio
Sep-Oct fires as % of Jun-Jul. Higher = more autumn activity.
💡 Hover to highlight. Notice the upward trend especially 2022-2025.
3. Geographic Migration
Latitude of fire activity center. Moving south = warmer regions with longer seasons.
💡 Southward shift from 35°N (2018) to 33°N (2025) = ~130 mile migration.
4. Fire Intensity by Month
Average power per fire detection (MW). Click months to show/hide.
💡 Click month buttons to toggle visibility. Recent years show more intense late-summer/fall fires.
5. Year Comparison: All Metrics
Compare temporal spread, late/early ratio, and geographic center for any year.
💡 Select a year to see detailed metrics for that year in context of all years.
How to Explore:
  • Brush: Drag across Chart 1 to filter all other views to a year range
  • Hover: Move mouse over any point to see exact values in tooltip
  • Toggle: Click month buttons (Chart 4) or trend line button (Chart 1) to show/hide elements
  • Compare: Select a year (Chart 5) to see its statistics highlighted across all metrics
  • Highlight: Hovering one view subtly highlights that year in others

Key Insight: The upward trend in temporal dispersion (Chart 1: 40.4 → 48.0 std dev) combined with growing late/early ratio (Chart 2) and southward geographic shift (Chart 3) provides strong evidence that fire seasons are lasting longer and extending into traditionally off-season months.
