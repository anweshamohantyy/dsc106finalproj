A Visual Investigation

Lightning Doesn't Light Most U.S. Wildfires

Folk belief blames lightning for wildfires. NOAA and the USDA say roughly 85 percent are human-caused. Here is what GOES-16 satellite data from the 2024 fire season shows about where lightning struck and where the country burned.

By: Anwesha Mohanty, Vipra Bindal, Dylan Cheng  ·  DSC 106 Final Project

One quick definition. Throughout this story, a "fire cell" is a piece of land roughly 50 km on a side (about the size of a U.S. county). We mark a cell as on fire that week if the GOES-16 satellite spotted active fire anywhere inside it during the week. Lightning strikes are counted inside the same cells. Source: NOAA GOES-16 ABI-L2-FDCC (fires) and GLM-L2-LCFA (lightning), 2024 fire season, public on AWS Open Data.

1 / Week by week

Watch a fire season unfold

Each circle is a fire cell: a county-sized patch of land (about 50 km on a side) where the GOES-16 satellite spotted active fire that week. Bigger circles mean more fire energy, measured in megawatts (MW): the same unit used to rate power plants. A backyard bonfire is fractions of a megawatt; a major wildfire can put out several thousand. Darker circles mean a hotter peak intensity. Drag the slider to walk from June through October.

2 / Stack the weeks

Now stack every week into one map

Every week of detection layered onto the same map. By the end of October, three burn bands have emerged: the Western mountains, the southern Plains corridor through Texas, and the long Mississippi Valley. The Northeast and Upper Midwest sit out the season almost entirely.

3 / Zoom out

Step back. Aggregate to states

The dots dissolve. Each state lights up by its total fire radiative power for the season. The geography is impossible to miss: a Western band of intense red, a Texas-to-Florida corridor of moderate orange, and the rest of the country quiet.

4 / Add the lightning

Now bring in the lightning

The map is recolored on two axes: fire vertically, lightning horizontally. Some states do appear dark in both directions. Texas, Louisiana, and the Mississippi Valley get agricultural burning and frequent Gulf thunderstorms, so they end up in the high-fire / high-lightning corner. But sharing a state isn't the same as one causing the other. Look at the loudest corners instead: Florida flashes the most lightning of any state and barely burns, while California burns hottest with little lightning to blame. The relationship is regional, not national. Click any state for the weekly breakdown.

5 / The verdict

Did the lightning actually come first?

One dot per fire cell, per week it burned. The X-axis is the number of lightning flashes in that same cell within the 24 hours before the fire was detected. If lightning caused fires, the dots would climb a diagonal. They don't. Drag any rectangle to focus a subset. The headline tells you how many had no preceding lightning. Whatever slice you take, the majority did.

6 / A new factor

Does atmospheric dryness explain what lightning doesn't?

Each state is now colored on two axes: fire intensity vertically, Vapor Pressure Deficit (VPD) horizontally. VPD measures how aggressively the atmosphere pulls moisture out of vegetation — the higher it is, the drier and more combustible the fuel becomes. Unlike lightning, which showed almost no correlation with fire intensity at the national level, VPD correlates with fire radiative power at r = 0.43. The Western states that burned the hardest sit squarely in the high-VPD column. Drag the slider to watch fire cells pulse through the season as atmospheric dryness rises and falls. Click any state for its weekly fire and VPD timeline.

Project Notes

Writeup

By: Anwesha Mohanty, Vipra Bindal, Dylan Cheng  ·  DSC 106 Final Project

Motivation

While most Americans think of lightning as the leading cause of wildfires, NOAA and the U.S. Forest Service have reported that up to 85% of them are caused by humans. This experiment explores that question by analyzing satellite data from GOES-16 and presents its findings in a way that reveals results that most Americans may find shocking. The proposition that we are exploring is that lightning causes most wildfires in the United States. Based on what we've found, it turns out the answer is no. Throughout the 2024 fire season, approximately 79% of U.S. wildfires had no lightning strikes in the 24 hours leading up to them, almost perfectly matching the federal reports.

Design Rationale

We initially broke down the visualization into a three-stage scrollytelling structure and ended up revising it to fit five stages. The five stages span from weekly fires, over aggregated seasons, to statewide analysis, a bivariate lightning overlay, and finally, a scatterplot-based conclusion. This structure allows viewers to build up a deeper understanding of the argument incrementally instead of being handed a conclusion with overwhelming data.

Most importantly, the fourth stage became necessary after a failed attempt to use a side-by-side map of both phenomena. Aligning two different color scales became too demanding, and there was no clear message to be gained from the visuals. We also chose to use a bivariate choropleth in a 3×3 palette in which red represents high fires and low lightning, and blue represents the opposite. This made the key insight immediately visible through color alone instead of requiring readers to interpret it themselves. We also considered a diverging map of fire divided by lightning, but combining two variables into a single ratio would likely suppress key information. The bivariate approach keeps both dimensions visible.

For color, fire uses an orange-red gradient that is physically intuitive, lightning uses yellow which matches how it appears in satellite imagery, and a cyan accent marks all interactive elements so they appear as a separate layer from the data rather than part of it. Deep purple is at the high right corner of the palette, and the lack of purple across the fire-heavy Western states also visually supports our overall conclusion.

All interactions were designed to stay consistent throughout. The slider reveals the wave of wildfire frequency across the continental U.S. over time, the state click-through provides additional checks for each state, and scatter brushing allows viewers to filter by lightning rate and watch the headline statistic update in real time.

Development Process

Our fire data was pulled from the GOES-16 ABI-L2-FDCC, binned by both date window and geographic cell size of 0.5 degrees, and filtered to include only detections marked as real fires based on the quality flag code. For lightning data, we sampled two files per hour from the GOES-16 GLM-L2-LCFA dataset and scaled the counts by 90× to estimate the full dataset. These values were only used for statewide aggregate analysis, where sampling noise becomes less significant. A fire cell was considered to have prior lightning activity if five or more lightning strikes were observed in the same cell within the preceding 24 hours. Single isolated strikes were excluded to reduce the impact of possible sensor artifacts.

We brainstormed roughly five other propositions before settling on this one because it was simple, fully supported by GOES data, and capable of being tested directly against the evidence. The hardest engineering challenge involved pulling the GLM data. The NOAA S3 bucket was heavily throttled at anonymous endpoints, and multiple sandboxed environments blocked outbound access entirely. We ultimately had to move the data pull into an unrestricted environment before replacing our mocked estimates with real lightning data. Once that happened, the headline statistic shifted to a more accurate 79%. Development took around 20 to 24 total people-hours (3 people × 8 hours).

All team members supported the development process consistently, with each team member playing key roles. Dylan primarily built the data pipeline, including the FDCC pull, GLM extraction and scaling, and the cross-referencing logic between fire cells and lightning activity. Vipra led most of the D3 visualization work, including the scrollytelling interaction, transitions, IntersectionObserver triggers, and the bivariate choropleth map. Anwesha led the narrative and visual design process, including proposition development, color palette selection, the per-state detail panel, and the final scatterplot visualization. The work was collaborative across all areas. All three team members contributed to debugging the GLM data issues and restructuring the project from a three-stage into a five-stage experience.
