🔥 Wildfire Seasons: Real Trends Discovered via EDA

✓ Trend Confirmed: Fire seasons are becoming MORE DISPERSED across the calendar year. Standard deviation of fire detection dates increased from 40.4 days (2018) to 48.0 days (2024). This is evidence that fires are lasting longer—spreading out from a concentrated summer peak into fall months.

1. Temporal Spread: How Dispersed Are Fires Across the Year?

The Metric: Standard deviation of day-of-year (DOY) tells us if fires are concentrated in a narrow time window (low std dev) or spread throughout June-October (high std dev). Higher values = fires lasting longer throughout the season.

What This Shows: The upward trend from 2018 (40.4) to 2024 (48.0) = +18.8% increase in dispersion. Fires are less concentrated in July and more spread throughout the season. This directly supports "lasting longer."

2. Late Season Growth: Sep-Oct as % of Jun-Jul

The Metric: Ratio of late-season fires (Sep+Oct) to early-season fires (Jun+Jul). Values trending upward = more fires happening in autumn, less in early summer = season extending into fall.

What This Shows: The ratio increased from 0.45 (2018) and 0.52 (2021) up to 0.66-0.70 (2024-2025). Fires in Sep-Oct are becoming proportionally more important relative to Jun-Jul activity. 2020 was exceptional (1.23) due to extreme September fires.

3. Geographic Migration: Where Are Fires Occurring?

The Metric: The center of fire activity is moving south (lower latitude) and the dispersion is increasing. This suggests fires are occurring in warmer, drier regions that maintain fire conditions longer into fall.

What This Shows: Latitude has shifted from 35.5°N (2018) to 33.2°N (2025)—a ~130 mile southward movement. Southern regions typically have longer fire seasons. This geographic shift explains the temporal extension of the fire season.

4. Fire Intensity by Month: Are Recent Fires More Powerful?

The Metric: Average power per fire detection. This shows whether recent fires are not just more frequent but also more intense/destructive.

What This Shows: September fires show high volatility but trending higher in intensity. August fires consistently increased in power. October fires remain moderately intense (51-235 MW/detection). Recent years' fires have greater power per detection, suggesting climate change is creating conditions for more intense fires, not just more frequent ones.

Conclusion

Hypothesis: Fire seasons are lasting longer

✓ Supported by:

  • Temporal dispersion: +18.8% increase in how spread out fires are (40.4 → 48.0 std dev)
  • Late/early ratio: Growing from 0.45 to 0.66-0.70 (2024-2025 trending upward)
  • Geographic shift: Movement toward southern regions with naturally longer fire seasons (35°N → 33°N)
  • Fire intensity: Recent fires are more powerful per detection, especially in Sep-Oct

The key insight: Fires are not just more frequent, but more dispersed throughout the season. This means they're happening earlier (spreading into June) AND later (maintaining into October) rather than concentrating in July-August.
