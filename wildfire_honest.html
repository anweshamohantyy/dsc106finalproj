🔥 Wildfire Seasons: What the Data Actually Shows

⚠️ Honest Assessment: The hypothesis that fire seasons are "starting earlier and lasting longer" is NOT clearly supported by this 8-year dataset. What we see instead is high month-to-month variability with no strong directional trend. Below are visualizations showing what's actually in the data, without cherry-picking.

1. The Real Story: Month-by-Month Volatility

Each month's fire count varies wildly year-to-year. Notice there's no consistent upward or downward trend—just noise. October ranges from 840 to 3,268. That's not a trend, that's chaos.

2. Box Plot: Median ± Range for Each Month

This shows the spread of values for each month across all years. The boxes show where most data falls. Big boxes = big variability. If seasons were "lasting longer," we'd expect October's box to be consistently higher than early years—it's not.

3. What Actually Changed: Total Season Intensity

Looking at the total fire count across all 5 months, 2018 was exceptional (29,703), then crashed in 2019-2022, recovered in 2023-2024. This suggests climate/weather variation year-to-year, not a systematic trend.

4. October Alone: No Clear Trend (With Error Bars)

If you only look at October, it LOOKS like there's growth from 2019→2024 (+105%). But that's cherry-picking the minimum point. 2018 had 3,268 October fires—higher than 2024's 1,728. The trend is unclear.

What We Can Actually Say

✗ NOT supported by this data:

  • Fires are starting earlier (we only have June-October data; no April/May baseline)
  • Fires are lasting longer (season is consistently June-October across all years)
  • October is systematically growing (too much year-to-year noise; 2018 was higher than recent years)

✓ ACTUALLY observed:

  • High variability: October ranges from 840→3,268 (287% swing). This suggests weather/climate variability dominates year-to-year changes.
  • 2018 was exceptional: Highest total fire count (29,703). All other years are lower.
  • No consistent pattern: If you plot October year-by-year, the line goes up and down randomly, not steadily upward.
  • To properly test the hypothesis: You'd need 30+ years of data (to average out weather noise) AND data from April/May (to detect earlier starts).

Visualization Approach

These visualizations use honest encodings:

  • Line charts: Show actual values without smoothing, making noise/variability visible
  • Box plots: Display full range and quartiles, not just means
  • No trend lines: Avoid implying false patterns where none exist
  • Multiple views: Same data shown different ways to prevent single-visualization bias
